Installing Windsurf and Getting Started with Agentic Coding

DSHB Professional Development Conference — June 24, 2026

Author

Dr. Adam Ross Nelson

Session 1 · June 24, 2026 · 2:00 – 3:10 pm

Step One: Read, Re-read, and Read Again

Before you do anything else, read the starter instructions on this page in their entirety. Then read them again. Then a third time. The single most common failure mode for groups in this assignment is skimming the starter material once, getting stuck at minute 20, and then having to backtrack. Twenty minutes of careful reading at the start saves an hour of frustration later.

What You’re Teaching

Windsurf is an agentic IDE built on top of VS Code. “Agentic” means the editor can take multi-step actions on your behalf — reading files, running commands, editing code across a project — based on natural-language instructions, and then return the result for you to review. Your peers will leave this session able to install Windsurf, configure it for a real project, and run their first agentic task.

Mini Quick-Start

Your group’s job is to walk through this entire flow yourselves before you teach it. Plan on at least one multi-hour solo session per group member, plus a group rehearsal.

1. Install Windsurf

Download Windsurf from the official site and install it on your operating system. Pay attention to the system requirements — older machines may need the lighter-weight build.

  • Official site: https://windsurf.com/
  • Confirm your install by opening Windsurf, signing in, and creating an empty project.

2. Open a Real Project

Clone a small public repository (one of your own from earlier coursework works well) and open it in Windsurf. The agentic features feel abstract on a blank project — they shine when there’s actual code to reason about.

3. Run Your First Agentic Task

Try one of these as your first prompt:

  • “Read the README and tell me what this project does.”
  • “Add a docstring to every function in analysis.R (or analysis.py).”
  • “Find any places where I’m hard-coding file paths and refactor them to use a config variable.”

Watch what the agent proposes before accepting any changes. The ability to inspect, accept, or reject diffs is the core skill.

4. Tour the Three Modes

Windsurf has more than one way to invoke the agent (chat, in-line edits, multi-file actions). Get comfortable switching between them. Your demo should show at least two.

Suggested Resources

  • Official docs: https://docs.windsurf.com/
  • Quick-start tutorial: linked from the Windsurf homepage; the “First-time user” walkthrough is the best place to start.
  • Anthropic / Claude resources: Windsurf integrates with Claude models; some peers may already have API keys from earlier in the program.

Suggested 70-Minute Lesson Outline

  • 0 – 10 min — Intro. Why this tool matters for behavioral data science, what your peers will be able to do by the end of the session, and a 60-second preview of the demo.
  • 10 – 25 min — Live demo. You at the front, narrating clicks and commands. Show one complete success path from a clean state. Don’t try to cover edge cases yet.
  • 25 – 55 min — Hands-on. Peers do the demo themselves on their own machines. You and your group circulate to help. This is where most of the real learning happens — protect this time.
  • 55 – 65 min — Troubleshooting and gotchas. Surface the things that tripped you up during your own learning. What error messages should peers expect, and what do they actually mean? OS-specific differences? Auth problems?
  • 65 – 70 min — Wrap. Where to go next, one or two stretch challenges, pointers to the official documentation, and a Q&A buffer.
Rehearse the timing

Run through the lesson with your group at least once before the real session. Most first-time presenters underestimate the live-demo portion and overestimate how much hands-on time they actually have. Cut content rather than rush.

On Using the Official Documentation (Not GenAI) as Your Primary Tutor

For this assignment, treat the tool creator’s official documentation as your primary teacher. There is a strong temptation to ask Claude, ChatGPT, or another generative AI to “just explain it to me.”

Relying on GenAI in this way during the earliest stages of adopting a new tool is a mistake. Learn from the documentation first, and then use GenAI to help you understand specific parts of the documentation or to clarify confusing concepts.

Here is why:

  • Docs are the authoritative source. GenAI tools paraphrase, summarize, and occasionally hallucinate. The official documentation is what the tool’s authors actually wrote about how the tool actually behaves.
  • Reading docs is the skill we’re teaching. The whole premise of this conference is that successful long-term scientists are the ones who can pick up a new tool from its documentation. If you outsource that to an AI, you miss the practice rep.
  • You’ll be a better teacher. When you’ve read the source material carefully, you can answer the questions your peers ask in the hands-on portion. When you’ve only skimmed an AI summary, you can’t.

Suggestion: When getting started out, use GenAI as a sidekick (to debug a specific error, to explain a single unfamiliar term). Do not use it as your tutor of first resort.

Topic-Specific Demo Tips

  • As you set Windsurf up for yourselves, keep track of common mistakes, errors, misunderstandings, and points of friction. Designate a member of your team to centrally collect sign-up issues, model-selection confusion, prompts that didn’t go where peers expected, and any IDE-specific quirks across Mac and Windows. Then write them up into a one-page “troubleshooting guide” you can share with the others in class on the day of via the course chat platforms.
  • Use a “team teaching” approach. To make this lesson successful, every single member of your team will need to be confident installing Windsurf, signing in, opening a project, writing a basic prompt, reading the proposed diff, and accepting or rejecting the agent’s suggestion. The members of your team will need to circulate through the room helping other students. There will be many stumbling blocks.
  • Have a fallback project ready. If the demo project has issues on the classroom Wi-Fi, you want a small local project you can pivot to without losing momentum.
  • Pre-stage your prompts. Live-typing prompts during a demo invites typos and dead air. Write your three or four demo prompts in advance and paste them in.
  • Show a rejection. Don’t just show prompts that work. Show one where the agent proposes something wrong, and demonstrate how you push back. This is one of the most valuable things peers can learn.
